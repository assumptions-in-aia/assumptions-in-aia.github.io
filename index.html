Examining Assumptions in the Algorithmic Impact Assessment

Canada's Algorithmic Impact Assessment is a tool designed to surface risk — but like any instrument, it carries assumptions of its own. This workshop investigates what those assumptions are, where they sit, and what practitioners, advocates, and the state can do about them.

About the Workshop

The AIA exists as a questionnaire that departments must complete before deploying automated decision systems. But completing or scrutinizing an AIA requires more than filling in answers — it requires understanding the assumptions baked into the questions themselves, the scoring logic, and the instrument's framing of what counts as risk.

This session takes the AIA as a given. Rather than evaluating whether the instrument should exist or how it ought to have been designed, the focus is on what can be done with the tool as it currently stands — by those who complete it, those who challenge it, and those who design it.

What to Expect

Participants will work through a structured framework for identifying and labeling assumptions in the AIA, applied to real questions from the instrument. The session is participatory — responses will be solicited either by speaking aloud or by scanning the QR code displayed on screen to submit anonymously.

A Working Framework

A label structure that classifies assumptions by where they sit and what acting on them makes possible.

Applied Analysis

Live examination of specific AIA questions and responses, with the framework applied in real time.

Concrete Outputs

For each assumption identified: a reconstructed question, a rephrased choice, or a practice for the respondent — depending on where the assumption lives.

Open Discussion

Space to challenge, extend, or push back on the framework and its application.

Who Should Attend

This session is designed for three kinds of participants, each of whom relates to the AIA differently.

Practitioners Public servants and teams who complete the AIA on behalf of a project, and who bear responsibility for how the instrument is filled out.
Advocates Those who scrutinize a completed AIA on behalf of affected parties, with the aim of identifying where assumptions obscure or misrepresent a system's actual impact.
The State Policy designers and researchers interested in how the instrument itself could be improved — through better questions, clearer scoring, or more explicit framing.
